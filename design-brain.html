case study · design infrastructure · digistay (current role)

i stopped only designing the screens. i started teaching the company's ai to design.

digistay runs an internal ai orchestrator — dozens of skills for operations, business rules and integrations. i wrote our design system into it as a skill of its own: 200+ components with priorities, brand rules, tokens, patterns. now anyone can prompt and get on-brand output. and when the fix is small, i don't file a ticket — i open the frontend repo and push the pr myself.

why it matters

every team asks design the same small questions. which component? what spacing? is this on-brand? the design system knows — but it's a document, and documents don't answer.

the orchestrator already knew the business — booking rules, integrations, cost guards. what it didn't have was taste. anything it helped build could be functional and still look wrong. that gap was mine to close: if the ai knows the design system, everyone has a designer on call.

the move

i wrote the design system as an agent skill — the same way engineers write business rules.

not a pdf, not a figma page with rules nobody reads on time. a skill in the company repo, versioned, committed, sitting in the orchestrator next to the operational skills. design now lives where the work actually happens.

inside the skill

the hard part wasn't listing components. it was teaching judgment.

brand.mdthe non-negotiables. voice, color, what digistay looks like and refuses to look like. everything the brain produces passes through this.
components.mdthe 200+ list, prioritised. not an inventory — a ranking. the ai reaches for the right component first, not any component that fits.
tokens.mdthe numbers behind consistency. color, type and spacing as values, so output is measurably on-system, not approximately.
grid-spacing.mdhow things sit. the rhythm that makes a screen feel like ours before you read a word of it.
patterns.mdhow screens compose. components are vocabulary; patterns are grammar — list pages, detail pages, modals that behave.
icons.md + assets/the physical vocabulary. what exists, so nothing gets invented twice.

writing it was its own audit: you cannot hand-wave a priority to a machine. every "it depends" i'd been carrying in my head had to become a rule i could defend.

design → dev

the same fluency runs the other way: when the fix is small, i ship it myself.

copy tweaks, spacing slips, token corrections — the fixes that die in backlogs because a ticket costs more than the change. i make them in the frontend repo and open the pr; engineering reviews and merges. design debt stops accumulating, and the b.tech finally pays rent.

where this goes · the design brain

the skill is the first organ. the brain is the plan.

what i'm building next: an agent with the company's full context — product, users, brand history — that thinks like a senior product designer and a creative head at once. cross-functional teams ask it first: marketing checks a layout, engineering checks a flow, sales mocks a screen for a demo. everything it produces routes through the brand rules before it ships. honestly labeled: in progress — and the parts that exist are already answering questions that used to wait for me.

decisions underneath

  1. write for the machine reader

    a design system pdf persuades humans; a skill instructs machines. same truths, different grammar — and the machine version never skims.

  2. priorities, not inventories

    200 components listed is noise. 200 components ranked is judgment. the ranking is the design work.

  3. branding is the gate

    whatever the brain produces, it passes through brand rules before anyone ships it. taste isn't optional at scale — it's enforced.

  4. design owns the small diffs

    a ticket for a 4px fix costs more than the fix. push the pr, let engineering review, keep the backlog for real work.

  5. grow an organ at a time

    one skill shipped and used beats a grand brain announced. the roadmap is incremental on purpose.
